**[Visual: Alibaba HQ → cut to Yungu School photo → Jack Ma + executives group photo]**

> On March 3rd, Jack Ma — the founder of Alibaba — did something unusual.
>
> It was the first day back at work after the new year. But instead of going to the office, he brought every single one of Alibaba's top executives to a school.
>
> Not to talk about business. To talk about AI.
>
> What he said is worth paying attention to — not because it's new, but because of who's saying it, and who was in the room.

---

## 📖 Part 1: What Happened（1 min）

**[Visual: Yungu School exterior, news headlines, executive headshots with names/titles]**

> Jack Ma showed up at Yungu School in Hangzhou — a school he founded that runs from kindergarten through high school.
>
> With him were Joe Tsai, Alibaba's Chairman. Eddie Wu, Alibaba's CEO. Jiang Fan, head of Alibaba's e-commerce group. And Eric Jing, Chairman of Ant Group.
>
> This is essentially Alibaba's entire leadership team, in one room, at a school, on day one of the new year.
>
> Their message to the teachers: AI is coming faster than anyone expected, and education needs to change — now.

---

## 📖 Part 2: Jack Ma's Core Message（2 min）

**[Visual: Key quotes appearing as text overlays, clean typography, Jack Ma photos]**

> Jack Ma made four key points.

> **First — the speed of change.**
>
> His exact words: "The AI era has arrived rapidly. The impact on society is beyond imagination. None of us are fully prepared."
>
> He emphasized that AI is now iterating on a weekly cycle. Not monthly. Not quarterly. Every single week, the capabilities are growing.

> **Second — society will restructure.**
>
> He said this technology revolution will be historic in its impact on productivity and society. In the future, people may not need to work eight hours a day. Wealth will be abundant — but many familiar job categories will disappear.

**[Visual: Elon Musk speaking at a conference, split screen with Jack Ma]**

> This is remarkably similar to what Elon Musk has been saying. Musk has stated in multiple interviews that "most jobs will be done by AI" and that "human work will become optional."
>
> Two of the most influential entrepreneurs on the planet, arriving at essentially the same conclusion, independently. That's worth noting.
>
> But Jack Ma went further. He said the disruption is massive — but so is the opportunity. Many entirely new professions will emerge that we can't even imagine today. It's not just about jobs disappearing. New things will grow.

> **Third — education must return to its essence.**
>
> This is where he spent the most time. He said the way to judge whether a school belongs to the AI era is not by how many AI servers it has, or how advanced its AI training programs are.
>
> He used a memorable line: "AI has chips. Humans have heart."
>
> The time spent on rote memorization and test prep can now be freed up — for creativity, imagination, music, art, and sports. Teachers can finally become what he called "engineers of the soul" — not just people who pour knowledge into students' heads.

> **Fourth — don't make kids compete with AI.**
>
> The future is not about children beating AI at calculation or memorization. Curiosity, imagination, creativity, judgment, and aesthetic sense — these are the real capabilities that education needs to develop.

---

## 📖 Part 3: Joe Tsai's Framework — Three Things That Make Us Human（2 min）

**[Visual: Joe Tsai photo, three items appearing one by one with icons]**

> What I found especially valuable was Joe Tsai's contribution. He took Jack Ma's philosophical message and gave it a more concrete framework.

> He said in the AI era, critical thinking becomes essential. And he made an important distinction — it's not about being able to ask questions. It's about asking the *right* questions.

> Then he and Eddie Wu outlined three core differences between humans and machines:

> **One — curiosity.**
>
> Curiosity is what drives humans to initiate. You see something new and you want to explore it, understand it, play with it. That impulse is self-driven. Machines, at least for now, are reactive. They wait for instructions.

> **Two — empathy.**
>
> The ability to understand another person — not just their words, but their feelings, their context, their unspoken concerns. This is something AI can simulate but doesn't truly possess.

> **Three — physical capability.**
>
> Eddie Wu put it bluntly: "When brainpower is replaced by AI, physical ability becomes very important. Sports will matter more and more in the future."

**[Visual: Neuralink chip diagram, Musk presenting Neuralink]**

> Now here's an interesting extension. Elon Musk is building Neuralink — a brain-computer interface. If that technology matures, the boundary between human and machine cognition starts to blur.
>
> Musk himself has said he expects this to begin within six to eight years.
>
> So the three advantages Joe Tsai outlined — curiosity, empathy, physical ability — may hold true for our generation. But further out? The line between human and AI may not be as clear as we think.

---

## 📖 Part 4: Why Now — The Alibaba Context（1 min）

**[Visual: Qwen3.5 announcement, Musk's tweet, Alibaba AI triangle diagram]**

> There's an important backdrop to all of this.
>
> The day before Jack Ma's school visit — March 2nd — Alibaba open-sourced four new Qwen 3.5 small language models.
>
> Elon Musk commented on social media that these small models achieved "an astonishing level of intelligence."
>
> At the same time, Alibaba unified all its AI products under the "Qwen" brand and revealed what's being called the AI Golden Triangle — Tongyi Lab for models, Alibaba Cloud for infrastructure, and T-Head for custom chips.
>
> Right now, only two companies in the world have top-tier capabilities across all three domains — models, cloud, and chips. That's Alibaba and Google.
>
> So Jack Ma choosing this moment to talk about AI and education isn't random. Alibaba's AI investment has reached a scale where the deeper societal questions can't be ignored.

---

## 📖 Part 5: What This Looks Like in Practice（1.5 min）

**[Visual: concrete scenario illustrations, simple animations]**

> All of this can feel abstract. So let me give two concrete examples of what "AI disruption plus opportunity" actually looks like.

> **Example one — agriculture.**
>
> Imagine you own a piece of farmland, but you can't afford to hire five workers. In places like Canada or Australia, labor costs are enormous. But with AI-powered agricultural robots, you could deploy twenty machines that work around the clock. You set the blueprint — this section grows vegetables, that section grows lavender, over here we raise fish — and they execute. You manage strategy. They handle labor. This isn't science fiction. It's already in trial.

> **Example two — wealth management.**
>
> This one is directly relevant to my own industry. Last week, Anthropic released wealth management plugins for Claude — their AI model. These plugins can analyze investment portfolios, detect drift from target allocations, identify tax-loss harvesting opportunities, and generate rebalancing recommendations.
>
> A traditional investment advisor spends hours each day manually reviewing client portfolios. AI can do this in seconds, without missing anything. This doesn't replace the advisor — it turns one advisor into ten. The human focuses on what machines can't do: understanding a client's fears, reading market sentiment, making judgment calls under uncertainty.
>
> This is exactly what Jack Ma meant. The disruption is real. But so is the opportunity — if you're willing to adapt.

## 📖 Part 6: My Perspective（1 min）

**[Visual: workspace setup, Signal daily digest screenshot (sanitized), simple system diagram]**

> I'll close with a personal note.
>
> I've worked in the financial industry for over a decade, and I also build with AI every day. A lot of what Jack Ma said resonates with what I experience firsthand.
>
> I built an AI-powered information system that scans hundreds of articles daily — from Hacker News, Reddit, ArXiv, GitHub Trending — and uses AI to score each one based on my specific interests and goals. Every few weeks, I need to update the system because the models have gotten better again. That's the "weekly iteration cycle" Jack Ma was talking about.
>
> Joe Tsai said asking the right question matters more than asking many questions. I feel this every day working with AI. Eighty percent of the outcome depends on how you frame the problem. Same tools, different questions — the results can be ten times apart.
>
> I think what Jack Ma did — choosing a school as Alibaba's first stop of the year — sends a powerful signal. AI isn't just a business transformation. It's a societal one. And how well we navigate it won't come down to the technology itself. It'll come down to how we think.
