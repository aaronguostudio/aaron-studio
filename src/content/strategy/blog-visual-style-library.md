# Aaron Blog Visual Style Library

## Purpose

This is the reusable style-family library for Aaron's blog images. It complements, but does not replace, `blog-visual-system.md`.

- Visual mode answers: what job does this image do?
- Style family answers: what visual language does this image speak?
- Style probe answers: what genuinely different directions should we test before committing?

Do not treat visual mode as style family. `Operator Diagram` can be rendered as an executive brief, a data poster, a cutaway system map, or a cartoon briefing. `Human-Scale Metaphor` can be rendered as paper editorial, documentary collage, or clean cartoon narrative.

## Current External Signals

Use external design trend sources as weak signals, not commands. Recent visual trend writing keeps pointing toward tactile surfaces, mixed media, editorial systems, strong typography, modular grids, bolder infographics, and more human or handmade texture. For Aaron's blog, translate that into clearer art direction, not noisier AI images.

Useful source families to revisit:

- [Adobe Express design trends](https://www.adobe.com/express/learn/blog/design-trends-2026)
- [Canva design trends](https://www.canva.com/design-trends/)
- [Figma design and product design writing](https://www.figma.com/blog/)
- [Venngage graphic design and infographic trend writing](https://venngage.com/blog/graphic-design-trends/)
- editorial and magazine design references
- high-quality presentation / briefing references

## Selection Rules

Pick style families by role:

- Cover can be louder, more editorial, and more atmospheric.
- Body images should usually be quieter and easier to read.
- Framework images should privilege scanning and reuse.
- Thumbnails can be more graphic than the article body.
- A style family should be rejected if it makes the image explain more than one idea.

For normal articles, prefer one primary family plus one or two functional variants. For explicit visual exploration, use Style Probe Mode and generate visibly different candidates before deciding.

## Style Families

| Style family | What it feels like | Best for | Use carefully when | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Field Signal Editorial | Warm-white editorial print, graphite structure, paper-cut objects, human pencil corrections, safety-color signals | Operational essays, deployment loops, evidence-led covers, quiet body metaphors | A post needs an authored, tactile alternative to glass | Dirty halftone, fake microcopy, retro-industrial nostalgia |
| Soft Glass Narrative | Flat editorial scenes with restrained glass accents and human-scale metaphors | Default blog body images, reflective tech essays | The post needs warmth and continuity | Turning every image into cyan glass ribbons |
| Editorial Workbench | Premium desk/workbench photography or rendered collage, paper, tools, stamps, runbooks, subtle traces | Covers, market arguments, operational essays | The article needs business credibility | Too much leather, finance luxury, dark boardroom energy |
| Infographic Editorial | Magazine-style infographic with large numbers, bold arrows, simple icons, limited copy zones | Explainers, comparison sections, framework summaries | The idea can be reduced to 3-5 visual chunks | Tiny text, consulting-slide clutter, fake charts |
| Executive Brief | Board memo / briefing slide aesthetic: crisp grid, one headline zone, 3-4 evidence blocks, restrained typography | Enterprise arguments, investment / strategy posts, article thumbnails | The article needs authority and scanability | Generic PowerPoint look, too many boxes |
| Cartoon Briefing | Clean editorial cartoon style with simplified people or objects, light humor, one visual punchline | Explaining awkward incentives, consultant-vs-FDE contrast, reader-friendly hooks | The topic can tolerate a lighter tone | Infantilizing serious ideas, mascot energy |
| Data Poster | Poster-like composition using one metric, one diagram, and high-contrast typography | Viral covers, thesis images, provocative claims | The article has a sharp quantitative or conceptual claim | Decorative numbers without substance |
| Cutaway System Map | Architectural cutaway or exploded-view system, showing layers and handoffs with few labels | Technical mechanisms, deployment loops, operating models | The reader needs to understand structure | Isometric SaaS explosions, too many nested layers |
| Paper System Sketch | Hand-drawn paper map, pencil arrows, sticky notes, notebook fragments, warm low-tech thinking | Early-stage frameworks, personal reflection, draft ideas | The article benefits from "thinking in public" | Messy sketch marks and unreadable handwriting |
| Editorial Photo Collage | Real object or place texture with restrained graphic overlay | Covers, high-reality business topics, breaking sameness | The post needs groundedness | Stock-photo meetings, fake smiles, random skylines |
| Bento Product Artifact | Modular grid of product-like cards, large symbols, minimal labels, polished UI artifact | Framework cards, reusable concepts, tool/process posts | The image needs to feel like a designed system | Dashboard walls and fake SaaS UI |
| Print / Risograph Accent | Slightly imperfect print texture, limited colors, bold shapes, editorial zine energy | Contrarian opinion pieces, cultural tech essays, visual variety | Used as an accent or cover experiment | Muddy textures, retro for its own sake |
| Abstract Mechanism | Minimal abstract geometry, one force relation, no literal workplace objects | Philosophical or conceptual sections | Literal imagery would feel too obvious | Generic gradients, meaningless orbs |

## Style Probe Mode

Use Style Probe Mode when Aaron asks to explore visual direction, compare styles, or make images "very different".

Rules:

- Generate 3-6 candidates for the same article, not a full final set.
- Each candidate must use a visibly different style family.
- At least one probe should be a quiet body-image direction.
- At least one probe should be a framework or infographic direction.
- At least one probe may be a louder cover / thumbnail direction.
- Do not replace final article images during probing.
- Write a short style-probe critique before choosing winners.

Recommended probe menu for enterprise / AI workflow essays:

| Probe | Style family | Role | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Infographic Editorial | framework or contrast | Tests scanability and shareability |
| B | Cartoon Briefing | hook or contrast | Tests warmth, clarity, and memorability |
| C | Executive Brief | cover or thesis | Tests authority and business seriousness |
| D | Cutaway System Map | mechanism | Tests whether the operating model can be made visual |
| E | Paper System Sketch | body metaphor | Tests a lighter human-thinking style |

## Critique Questions

Ask these before promoting a probe into the final set:

- Does the style clarify the article's argument or merely look fresh?
- Can this style repeat across 2-3 images without fatigue?
- Is the image readable in five seconds?
- Does it avoid generic AI, dashboard, or stock-photo energy?
- Does it feel like Aaron's blog, or like an unrelated template?
- Would it help video animation or make it harder?
