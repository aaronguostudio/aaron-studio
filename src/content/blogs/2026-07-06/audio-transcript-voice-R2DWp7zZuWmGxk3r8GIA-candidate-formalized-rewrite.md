## A Seven

The numbers don’t line up neatly. OpenAI reported an investment in a new company, while AWS and Microsoft talked about their organizational commitments. I see that seven and a half billion dollars as a scale indicator, not a direct spending comparison.

The common focus here is evident. These companies are working on increasing deployment capacity.

Models can generate code, analyze documents, and interact with tools, but they can't independently manage a company's data, handle permissions, secure approvals, establish recovery paths, or assign responsibilities effectively for a reliable production system.

That gap highlights the significance of forward deployed engineering.

## Inside The Factory

A manufacturer has a serious problem: a lost supplier.

Supplier data sits in one system. Production capacity is tracked in another. Customer orders are in yet another place. Approval rules depend heavily on the expertise of experienced employees.

The key question is identifying which orders are now at risk. What options do planners have to address these risks? Who has the authority to approve any changes?

A forward deployed engineer works alongside the operators to make decisions. They connect the data, identify key objects, and determine necessary actions. This involves setting up permissions, approvals, exceptions, and planning for recovery from the beginning. 

This is deployment work.

## Human Backpropagation

Palantir refers to this as forward deployed engineering, likening it to the human equivalent of backpropagation. While that sounds academic, the work itself is practical and direct.

A planner spots an exception. A data source is delayed. An approval path stops the wrong person from moving ahead. The engineer fixes the local issue, but then asks: will this failure happen again elsewhere?

If the answer is yes, focus back on the product. The next deployment should include an improved connector, a refined permission model, a fresh evaluation, or a new workflow primitive.

This keeps field work from becoming an endless cycle of custom services.

## Five Minutes Is The Wrong Story

I encounter a smaller version of this in my own publishing workflow. 

When someone claims AI can produce a solid article in five minutes, they’re overlooking a significant amount of effort involved.

My first workflow brought together research, outlining, writing, translation, images, audio, video, and publishing. It was quick and efficient. However, it also had a tendency to carry weak evidence, awkward language, repetitive visuals, and broken links right through to the final product.

The second version demanded more engagement. I needed to reread, reject, and challenge claims. I compared visual directions and tested the final package. Quality improved as those judgments turned into evidence gates, editorial rules, and validators.

While this doesn’t confirm that FDE operates effectively at the enterprise level, it does illustrate a similar working pattern: staying close to actual work, identifying gaps in general capability, and integrating corrections back into the system.

## Not Consulting With A New Name

It's an oversimplification to label FDE as just consulting under a different name.

Experienced consultants and systems integrators grasp workflows, oversee change, navigate regulations, and manage complex transformations. Forward deployed engineers focus on operational execution, not on those broader skills.

The key difference lies in how field work connects to the product. A solid FDE team remains aligned with production and integrates directly with core engineering.

An engagement that results in custom code, a handoff, and more reliance on the vendor doesn’t show progress. Real value comes when a customer can operate the system on their own, and field learning improves the platform.

## The Customer Must Keep The Workflow

That proximity introduces a risk. 

A vital workflow can gradually become dependent on a single vendor’s connectors, evaluations, permission model, agent runtime, and underlying assumptions about how tasks should be executed.

Deployment can appear effective, but the customer's ability to grasp or adapt to it often diminishes.

The customer must keep the essential components: data contracts, evaluation sets, permission rules, audit history, runbooks, failure modes, and the interface between the workflow and the model.

A productive engagement builds the customer’s capabilities instead of creating a dependency on a vendor.

## ACTOR Is The Deployment Test

I use a framework called ACTOR, which is similar to the word "actor." 

It outlines five questions I need to answer to assess the success of an AI deployment.

What specific decision, handoff, or task will create change? 

Focus on what the system needs to know and what access it has.

Trust relies on understanding AI's role. Is it drafting, recommending, deciding, or acting? Who's responsible for approving its actions? If it fails, what are the consequences?

For outcomes, we need clear evidence that the work has led to improvement. What benchmarks or metrics will we use to measure success?

Recursive: how does this run benefit the workflow and the next run?

That last word is key. A loop in a diagram doesn’t guarantee self-improvement. It depends on someone spotting failures, assessing their importance, and turning those lessons into improved evaluations, rules, runbooks, and tools.

The goal isn’t to set up continuous manual tasks. It’s about improving our ability to make sound judgments over time.

## Tokens Are Inputs

Token usage shows your consumption of a model. However, it doesn't indicate whether decisions improved, workflows became faster, or if the organization gained meaningful insights.

The largest AI platforms are establishing deployment organizations. This effort aims to reduce the gap between intelligence and operations.

FDE may evolve, but the core principle remains unchanged. 

When evaluating AI, consider its effect on workflows and the skills it offers the customer. 

The crucial question is whether those costly tokens lead to an operational system that the company genuinely controls.
