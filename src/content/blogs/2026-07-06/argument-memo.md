# Argument Memo

## Thesis

Enterprise AI is no longer bottlenecked mainly by model access. It is bottlenecked by deployment capability: the ability to turn intelligence into trusted operating loops inside real organizations.

## Why Now

The timing is visible because several major AI platforms made similar organizational moves in a short window:

- Anthropic created an enterprise AI services company.
- OpenAI launched the OpenAI Deployment Company.
- AWS committed $1B to an FDE organization.
- Microsoft launched Frontier Company with 6,000 embedded experts.

The pattern appears because enterprises have moved past curiosity. They have pilots, API access, copilots, and budget pressure. The harder question is whether AI changes cycle time, cost, quality, risk, revenue, or decision-making without breaking trust.

## Mechanism

Models create potential intelligence. Deployment converts that potential into operating capacity by solving five practical problems:

1. The work has to be specific enough to change.
2. The system needs the right context and data access boundaries.
3. The trust model has to match the autonomy level.
4. The outcome has to be measurable.
5. The owner and feedback loop have to survive after launch.

FDE matters because it puts engineers where those problems become visible: inside customer workflows, near production constraints, close to users, and close enough to product teams to generalize field learning.

## Evidence Map

- Four-company convergence: Anthropic, OpenAI, AWS, Microsoft.
- Palantir historical precedent: FDSE as one customer / many capabilities; field learning feeds product.
- AWS explicit consulting contrast: business outcomes, self-sufficiency, runbooks, internal champions.
- Microsoft trust layer: customer IQ, model choice, IP protection, continuous improvement.
- Fractional AI acquisition: applied AI implementation talent as scarce asset.
- Aaron's own AI workflow experience: value appears when intent, run, review, memory, and evidence are connected.

## Counterargument

This could be consulting with new branding, and model companies may simply be moving downstream to capture services revenue. Some FDE work could become expensive custom integration that does not scale.

Response: that risk is real, which is why the important distinction is not the title. The useful distinction is whether the work leaves behind production systems, internal capability, reusable patterns, and a feedback loop. FDE is valuable only when it compresses learning between customer workflow, engineering implementation, and product evolution.

## Reusable Frame

ACTOR:

- Action: what work changes?
- Context: what must the system know, and what are the boundaries?
- Trust: what can the system safely do?
- Outcome: what proves it improved the work?
- Recursive ownership: who owns the system, and how does it learn?

## Implication

For technical people, the durable career signal is not "learn prompting." Prompting is one interface. The deeper skill is becoming deployment-native: understanding work, integrating models into systems, designing evals and guardrails, measuring outcomes, and making every deployment improve the next one.
