# Idea

## Topic

Why Anthropic, OpenAI, AWS, and Microsoft are all moving toward forward deployed engineering and enterprise AI deployment services.

## Why Now

In May-July 2026, several major AI platforms announced unusually similar moves:

- Anthropic formed an AI services company with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, Goldman Sachs, and other investors.
- OpenAI launched the OpenAI Deployment Company and agreed to acquire Tomoro.
- AWS announced a $1 billion Forward Deployed Engineering organization.
- Microsoft launched Frontier Company with a $2.5 billion investment and 6,000 embedded industry and engineering experts.

This is not just "AI companies adding services." It is a signal that enterprise AI value is moving from model access to deployment capability.

## Target Reader

Technical operators, senior engineers, product leaders, engineering managers, founders, and AI-native builders who want to understand what enterprise AI will require after the demo stage.

## Reader Pain / Curiosity

Readers are hearing contradictory claims:

- AI models are getting dramatically better.
- Enterprise AI pilots still struggle to turn into production value.
- Consultants, systems integrators, and model companies are all claiming to own the AI transformation layer.
- FDE is suddenly becoming a visible role, but many people do not know how it differs from consulting, sales engineering, or product engineering.

The curiosity hook: why are so many AI companies doing the same thing at the same time?

## Initial Thesis

As models become more capable and more available, the scarce enterprise AI skill is no longer access to intelligence. It is the ability to deploy intelligence into real workflows with context, trust, measurable outcomes, ownership, and a learning loop.

## Why This Should Exist

The current draft already has a strong market pattern and a useful ACTOR framework. The 2.0 revision should make it more valuable by:

- making the personal operator angle sharper;
- reducing news-summary sections;
- making FDE a signal rather than the whole topic;
- giving technical readers a practical framework they can apply;
- connecting this essay to Aaron's previous writing about AI operating systems, one-person projects, and return distance.

## Kill Criteria

Do not publish if the piece reads like:

- a roundup of corporate announcements;
- a generic "AI implementation matters" essay;
- an unfair dunk on consultants;
- a finance/private-equity analysis outside Aaron's expertise;
- a shallow FDE career explainer without a stronger operating insight.
