# Visual Strategy: Human Backpropagation

## Article Character

Enterprise / market argument with a direct FDE opening, one grounded field-work mechanism, one personal workflow example, and one actionable framework.

## Reader Emotion

Start with friction and recognition, move into a clean "I had not seen FDE this way" insight, then end with control. The set should feel editorial, intelligent, and useful, without reading like a consulting deck.

## Visual Risk

- Repeating the previous pale infographic set until every section feels interchangeable.
- Reducing human backpropagation to a generic circular arrow.
- Using fake readable UI or a literal 404 screen as the whole visual idea.
- Turning ACTOR into five crowded cards.
- Letting token stacks, arrows, and workflow boxes appear in every image.
- Adding glass or glow merely to signal AI.

## Argument Beats That Need Images

| Beat | What the image clarifies | What is lost without it |
|---|---|---|
| Blog workflow v1 to v2 | A polished output chain still needs review and recursive improvement. | The personal explanation of human backpropagation remains verbal and easy to skim past. |
| FDE field work | An engineer and operator turn a supply disruption into a governed action and return the lesson to product. | FDE remains an abstract feedback metaphor rather than visible work. |
| FDE and consulting | Both can help the customer, but FDE remains connected to production and product engineering. | The distinction can sound like a semantic argument about job titles. |
| ACTOR | Five questions map to five deployment artifacts, with R returning to A. | The framework is less reusable and less memorable. |
| Closing value unit | Tokens become value only inside a customer-owned workflow. | The ownership argument and title have no visual payoff. |

## Selected Concept Route

`Agent-selected: A. Dead-End Meter`, refined with the tactile paper and print language of Route B.

Reason: it earns the current title immediately while leaving room for human backpropagation as the reveal. Route B becomes the mechanism image; Route C can inform the closing image without carrying the full cover.

## Recommended Mode Mix

- Cover: Editorial Minimal + Print / Risograph Accent, loud.
- Opening body: Human-Scale Metaphor, quiet.
- Mechanism: documentary editorial collage of active field work, medium.
- Comparison: Executive Brief, medium.
- Framework: Operator Diagram + Infographic Editorial, medium.
- Closing: Editorial Minimal, quiet.

## Cover Direction

One horizontal relation: expensive amber token blocks enter from the left and stop at a precise coral dead end; a human-drawn cyan/green feedback path closes the circuit and turns the right side into one verified operating action. Use no words, logos, dashboards, or literal robots.

## Body Image Direction

Use different compositions for different jobs: an editorial still life for the evolving blog workflow, a documentary factory cutaway for FDE, a map-to-engine comparison for consulting, a typographic system for ACTOR, and a keyed workflow press for the closing. Share palette and print texture, not layout. The four-output image remains an editorial cut.

## Framework / Diagram Direction

Use large typographic anchors and at most five major elements. The ACTOR image may contain the exact words Action, Context, Trust, Outcome, Recursive. The return from R to A is the only strong framework arrow. FDE is now shown through work at a factory planning table rather than a three-zone diagram.

## What To Avoid

- No vendor logos, brains, robots, neural-network heads, cloud icons, or glowing server rooms.
- No dark blue/purple AI palette.
- No full-scene liquid glass.
- No tiny labels, fake charts, random decorative numbers, or stock meetings.
- No two images may repeat the same left-to-right cards composition.
- No more than one image may use a stack of tokens.
