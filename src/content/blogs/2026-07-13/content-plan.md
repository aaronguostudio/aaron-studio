# Content Plan: The AI Revolution Hasn't Reached the Organization Yet

## Voice Check

**Positioning:** Ship with AI, not about AI — builder who ships, not commentator.  
**Voice rule:** Use “I” for lived experience; use sources for historical and economic claims; never turn a personal workflow into a universal prediction.  
**This post's personal anchor:** My own work changed when I stopped asking AI to complete isolated tasks and started designing systems that could execute, report back, be reviewed, and retain useful memory. I want to know whether that is merely a private productivity trick or the early shape of a wider organizational shift.

## Hook / Opening

**Blog hook:**

The day a company gives everyone ChatGPT is not the day it becomes an AI company. A century ago, factories made the same mistake with electricity: they replaced the steam engine with an electric motor, then kept the shafts, belts, layouts, and habits built for steam. The machine arrived. The system did not change.

I have felt a smaller version of this shift in my own work. My unit of work is no longer “I finish a task.” It is increasingly “I design a system that can execute, report back, be reviewed, and learn.” That made me ask a historical question: are we late to AI, or early to the reorganization that makes AI matter?

**X thread hook (tweet 1):**

AI has reached most offices. It has not reached most organizations. A century ago, factories installed electric motors but kept the steam-era belts and workflows. The productivity boom came later—after the factory was redesigned. AI may be at the same threshold.

## Core Argument / Thesis

Electricity and computers became transformative only after firms reorganized work around them; AI’s biggest contest is not who buys the best model, but who first turns model capability into reliable, connected, auditable work systems. That leaves real room for careers and new businesses—but no guarantee that gains will be broad or that every AI investment will work.

## Narrative architecture

The essay follows one question through three eras: **what happens when a general-purpose technology enters a system built for an older one?**

It uses a repeated sequence rather than a chronological history lecture:

1. New capability enters an old system.
2. Local efficiency appears, but coordination costs remain.
3. Complementary assets and new organization determine whether gains scale.
4. Society then decides how gains diffuse and who receives them.

## Outline

### Prologue: The machine arrived—and nothing changed

- Open in the present: individual AI use is conspicuous; most meetings, approvals, data handoffs, ownership structures, and incentives still look pre-AI.
- Make the original claim in the first 150 words: we may be early in AI’s reorganization, not late to its invention.
- Introduce the personal anchor in two short paragraphs, then move outward; do not begin as a memoir.
- Evidence note: use Census/NBER business adoption data only after defining “use,” not as a generic adoption headline.

### 1. Electricity’s false start: a motor pulling an old belt

- Show the steam-era power architecture: one power source, shafts, belts, fixed layout, all-or-nothing shutdown.
- Use the Kregel factory archive image as the visual proof of an intermediate state: electric motor, old line-shaft logic.
- Explain line-shaft → group drive → unit drive and why the main gain was flexibility, layout, material flow, maintenance, and scale—not merely lower energy cost.
- Include the crucial correction: modern causal research finds rapid gains in some energy-intensive places. The “delay” is not a universal waiting period.
- **Personal beat:** an AI tool that drafts faster but creates review queues is the contemporary version of a new motor attached to an old belt.

### 2. Ford’s achievement—and its cost—was a new system of relationships

- Show Highland Park as a systemization story: standard parts, specialized machinery, moving lines, flow of materials, pacing, training, and electrified flexibility.
- Explicitly state that Ford did not invent every component of the assembly line.
- Show what the history costs workers: more centralized pacing, repetitive tasks, high turnover, and a wage/discipline system designed to stabilize the labor force.
- Bridge to home and society: homes and farms electrified on a slower schedule because wires, finance, public institutions, standards, and appliances had to arrive too.
- **Reader takeaway:** productivity is not the same question as autonomy, equality, or a good life.

### 3. Computers gave the pattern a name

- Introduce Solow’s productivity paradox and Paul David’s “dynamo and computer” analogy.
- Describe the computer-era equivalent of the old belt: digitizing a paper workflow without changing decisions, information flow, accountability, or customer value.
- Use one system case (prefer Dell or P&G) to show that the winning innovation was not a computer purchase; it was the integration of data, inventory, decisions, and organizational boundaries.
- Bring in IT/organization/skill complementarity and 1990s productivity acceleration—then give the Gordon/Basu counterargument that this was not a universal or automatic result.
- **Personal beat:** links to Aaron’s past experience of persistent agents and distributed work memory, without claiming it proves a macro trend.

### 4. AI is not electricity. That is why this is harder.

- Make the distinction clear: electricity reorganized power; computers reorganized information; AI can reorganize parts of cognitive work.
- Introduce the four gates:
  1. Capability works on a task.
  2. Workflow can chain tasks, data, and exceptions.
  3. Organization can reassign roles, decision rights, metrics, and accountability.
  4. Society can diffuse it through infrastructure, skills, law, finance, and demand.
- Explain unique AI friction: model errors, privacy, copyright, security, context, auditability, and the fact that responsibility cannot be delegated to a probability distribution.
- Use Census/OECD data to show that many firms are still between gates one and two.

### 5. The evidence does not yet allow a victory speech

- Establish the optimistic case: credible task-level gains in customer support; models may let adjacent tasks chain into nonlinear organizational gains; AI could accelerate R&D.
- Establish the cautious case: email time can fall without task redesign; mature software systems can absorb AI output in review/verification costs; early firm surveys show broad use but limited measured impact.
- Pair the enterprise coding RCT with the METR RCT: do not ask “does AI work?” Ask “for which task, context, skill level, quality standard, and system boundary?”
- Use Acemoglu’s macro calibration as a disciplined counterweight, not an anti-AI verdict.
- State Aaron’s position: the decisive question is whether organization and diffusion can outrun concentration, friction, and mismatched incentives.

### 6. Career after the job description

- Avoid listing “safe jobs.” Jobs are bundles of tasks, context, relationships, and responsibility.
- Establish ILO’s distinction between exposure and job loss; use task transformation, not apocalypse rhetoric.
- Offer the durable five-part career stack:
  - define a valuable problem in a real domain;
  - supply context and constraints the model does not possess;
  - design reliable task chains and feedback loops;
  - judge quality, handle exceptions, and own the outcome;
  - earn trust across people, customers, and institutions.
- Make the advice practical, not moralistic: invite the reader to map one slow task chain in their own work, identify human handoffs, and run a bounded redesign experiment.

### 7. Investing in a reorganization, not a slogan

- State scope boundary: this is a valuation and value-capture framework, not investment advice or a list of tickers.
- Layer 1 — infrastructure: chips, networks, data centers, electricity, cooling. Early demand is real; risks include capital intensity, utilization, depreciation, grid bottlenecks, regulation, and price already paid.
- Layer 2 — adoption enablers: data, identity, permissions, cybersecurity, integration, evaluation, and vertical workflows. These assets help firms cross gates two and three.
- Layer 3 — AI-native value creators: companies that use a different operating model to create new products and demand. Most upside and most uncertainty live here.
- Use 1920s utilities and the dot-com era as the warning: technological truth, company success, and investor return are different propositions.
- Close this section with a scorecard: evidence of end-to-end workflow adoption, customer value, retention, unit economics, capital intensity, and valuation—not announcement volume.

### Epilogue: Early in the reorganization, not late to the invention

- Return to the opening line: a machine changes nothing by itself; a society decides whether it becomes a new system.
- Offer conditional hope. The model race may already be concentrated, but industries, workflows, norms, training systems, and customer expectations are still being designed.
- Be explicit: not everyone benefits automatically; some entry-level pathways may shrink, and gains can concentrate.
- The hopeful conclusion: there is still room for people who understand a real domain deeply enough to redesign work, build trust, and create new demand—not merely consume a tool.
- **Blog CTA:** “What is one task chain in your work that is clearly built for the old system? Reply—I want to collect the real examples.”
