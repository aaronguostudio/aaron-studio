# Video Brief: The One-Person Project

## Video Angle

This video is not "the blog in narration form." It is about the moment AI coding makes the old software delivery assembly line feel too slow, told through the story of a QA strategy shift and a new owner-led workflow, so the viewer leaves with a practical operating model: single owner, shared boundary.

## Target Audience

Product leaders, engineering managers, senior developers, and AI-native builders who already believe AI coding makes individuals faster, but are struggling with QA, review, and team alignment.

## Desired Emotion

Clear and slightly relieved: the viewer should feel that the chaos they are seeing is not a personal failure, but a predictable shift in the unit of collaboration.

## Core Promise

By the end, the viewer will understand why AI coding makes review and ownership more important, and how to reorganize collaboration around owner, agents, boundary, and evidence.

## Title/Thumbnail Expectation

Thumbnail/title promise: "The One-Person Project" and "AI coding changes collaboration."

The first 30 seconds must answer: What does "one-person project" mean, and why should a software team care?

## High-Shock Facts

- **9/10:** We changed QA strategy not because QA mattered less, but because AI made the old cadence mismatch obvious.
- **8/10:** AI can generate code, tests, docs, and PR summaries faster than humans can rebuild context.
- **8/10:** DORA found AI can increase individual productivity while exposing downstream stability and delivery risks.
- **8/10:** The new bottleneck is not typing code; it is review bandwidth.
- **7/10:** Multiple people using AI on the same project can increase coordination cost if nobody owns the full loop.
- **9/10:** "One-person project" is not solo work; it is recombined accountability.
- **8/10:** QA shifts from late gatekeeping into risk and evidence design.
- **9/10:** The useful management question changes from "which role does this?" to "who owns this end to end?"

## Hook Type

**Contradiction hook.**

Target: AI coding seems like it should make teams faster.

Transformation: It can make traditional team handoffs more expensive.

Stakes: If teams do not redesign ownership and review, faster code generation becomes slower delivery and more drift.

## Story Structure

**Personal operator story -> tension -> model -> proof -> objection -> payoff.**

This structure fits because the topic is strongest when grounded in a real workflow shift, then lifted into an operating model.

## Story Spine

1. Cold open: We changed QA strategy because AI broke the old delivery rhythm.
2. Promise: The one-person project is not anti-team; it is the new unit of accountability.
3. Old flow: BA -> design -> developer -> QA -> UAT -> deploy worked when execution was expensive.
4. Bottleneck: AI compresses execution, but human review bandwidth does not scale.
5. Model: One owner with agents inside shared team boundaries.
6. Objection: This can go wrong; without boundaries it becomes hero culture and system drift.
7. Payoff: Strong teams will ask four questions: who owns it, which agents, where are the boundaries, what evidence lets us ship?

## What The Video Adds

- A clearer spoken definition of "one-person project" in the first 30 seconds.
- A more concrete "day in the workflow" contrast: old handoff chain vs. AI-assisted owner loop.
- A sharper management lens: accountability architecture, not headcount reduction.
- A video-native payoff framed as four questions a team can use immediately.
