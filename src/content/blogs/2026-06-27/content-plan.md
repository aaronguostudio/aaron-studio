## Core Argument / Thesis

The frontier of a system is often revealed less by its official goal than by how far it can still operate after the plan breaks.

## Outline

### Section 1: A Record With a Strange Origin
- Explain Artemis II's 2026 record and Apollo 13's previous mark.
- Emphasize the contradiction: the old frontier was produced by an emergency.
- Avoid trivia tone; this is the doorway into the deeper question.

### Section 2: Two Kinds of Distance
- Apollo 13's distance was crisis distance: a route shaped by explosion, constraint, and return.
- Artemis II's distance was deliberate distance: a planned crewed test, a rehearsal for a long-term program.
- Same physical measure, different institutional meaning.

### Section 3: Failure Reveals the True Boundary
- A plan shows where we intend to go; failure shows what the system can survive.
- Apollo 13 became a test of engineering margin, communication, improvisation, and trust.
- This is why failure can expose capabilities that success hides.

### Section 4: The Operator's Frame
- For builders and teams, the useful lens is not "failure is good."
- The lens is: plan, margin, memory.
- Plan defines direction; margin creates survivability; memory turns accidents into institutional capability.

### Section 5: The Risk of Romanticizing Failure
- Apollo 13 should not be turned into a motivational poster.
- Failure is expensive and sometimes fatal. The point is not to seek failure.
- The point is to design systems that can learn, recover, and continue when reality diverges from the plan.

### Conclusion: What Counts as Far
- Artemis II makes the old record historical instead of current.
- The deeper lesson remains: distance is not only geography. It is also the radius of a system's judgment under pressure.
