# Soft Glass Narrative Visual System Design

## Purpose

Create a durable Aaron Studio blog illustration language that feels modern, artistic, recognizable, and easy to read.

The previous direction, `Signal Glass Atlas`, established useful materials and motifs, but the first generated set became too dense: too many glowing dashboards, charts, routes, nodes, and system diagrams. That density makes a blog post feel like a technical research deck.

The new system keeps the premium glass material and workflow intelligence, but shifts the default from "explain the whole system" to "carry one emotional or conceptual idea per image."

## Selected Direction

Use **Soft Glass Narrative** as the default Aaron blog visual language.

One-line definition:

```text
Soft Glass Narrative uses flat editorial composition, human-scale scenes, symbolic metaphors, and restrained liquid-glass materials to make AI-native work feel understandable, personal, and premium.
```

## Design Inputs

The direction combines:

- Apple's Liquid Glass as a material reference: translucent depth, light passing through layers, soft refraction, and content-aware surfaces.
- Microsoft Fluent as a system reference: hierarchy, depth, spacing, legibility, and restraint.
- Atlassian-style illustration discipline: intentional visuals, clear narrative purpose, no decorative complexity.
- IBM Carbon-style AI restraint: AI visuals need a recognizable visual vocabulary, but should remain contextual and usable rather than theatrical.
- Aaron's own blog context: personal stories, agent workflows, creative systems, self-improvement loops, and a long-term personal brand.

## Core Problem To Solve

AI blog visuals tend to collapse into the same generic language:

- dark blue glowing dashboards;
- abstract networks;
- humanoid robots;
- dense flowcharts;
- fake unreadable UI;
- cyberpunk 3D depth;
- too many charts and nodes.

That language can look impressive in isolation, but it is tiring inside a long-form blog post. Aaron's visual system should make readers feel clarity, taste, and human relevance, not visual fatigue.

## Visual Principles

### 1. Human-Scale First

Every image should start with a human-scale scene, object, gesture, or metaphor:

- a hand holding a phone;
- a desk with quiet work continuing;
- a car window and a road splitting ahead;
- a folder becoming a garden path;
- a room that organizes itself while a person walks outside;
- a notebook with translucent layers above it;
- a family scene where AI is background infrastructure.

The AI system should be present as a second layer, not always the subject.

### 2. One Idea Per Image

Each body image should communicate one sentence. If an image needs five clauses to explain, it is too dense.

Good:

```text
Delegated work can keep moving while life stays in the foreground.
```

Too much:

```text
Codex routes Notion tasks through skills, agents, subagents, cloud nodes, local workstations, metrics, review gates, and memory graphs.
```

### 3. 70 / 20 / 10 Density Budget

For a typical article image set:

- 70% narrative or metaphor images;
- 20% light conceptual diagrams;
- 10% dense system/framework diagrams.

For an 8-image body set, this means:

- 5 to 6 narrative/metaphor images;
- 1 to 2 light diagrams;
- 0 to 1 dense framework diagram.

### 4. Flat Editorial Base, Liquid Glass Accent

The default style should be closer to high-end editorial illustration than 3D render.

Use:

- flat or softly dimensional shapes;
- clean silhouettes;
- restrained gradients;
- soft transparent sheets;
- subtle refraction;
- large calm areas;
- clear object hierarchy.

Avoid:

- full 3D dashboard rooms;
- neon bloom;
- sci-fi HUD overlays;
- isometric SaaS explosions;
- glowing blue grids;
- excessive micro-panels.

### 5. AI As Atmosphere, Not Always UI

The AI layer can be shown through:

- a soft glass ribbon;
- a small light path;
- a translucent assistant shadow;
- papers arranging themselves;
- a route quietly continuing in the background;
- a window reflection;
- a soft green checkpoint;
- a subtle cyan thread.

Do not always show AI as screens, charts, or control panels.

## Style System

### Name

**Soft Glass Narrative**

### Sub-Modes

#### A. Soft Glass Editorial

Default mode.

Use for covers and most body illustrations. Human scene plus one symbolic AI layer.

#### B. Humanized System Fables

Use for personal, reflective, or philosophical sections. More metaphorical and lightly surreal, but still modern.

#### C. Neo Diagram Minimal

Use only when the reader needs a mechanism. One clean diagram, low density, large shapes, no chart wall.

## Palette

Use warm, balanced, low-noise colors:

- ink graphite for contrast;
- warm white for paper and glass;
- soft cyan for delegated AI traces;
- warm amber for human intent and life direction;
- soft green for review or trust;
- muted coral only for risk or friction;
- dusty violet sparingly for creative/media work.

Avoid a dominant dark-blue AI palette. Avoid pure purple gradients, beige-only creator palettes, and high-saturation neon.

## Material Rules

Liquid glass is an accent material:

- one to three glass elements per image;
- broad translucent shapes, not dozens of micro-panels;
- glass should improve metaphor or hierarchy;
- keep the main subject readable without the glass layer;
- no interface clutter inside glass unless the image is intentionally a diagram.

## Composition Rules

- Prefer 1 primary subject, 1 supporting object, and 1 AI-material accent.
- Use negative space as a quality signal.
- Avoid full-bleed complexity in body images.
- Keep important objects large enough to understand at blog width.
- Use diagrams only when the section introduces a reusable model.
- Use scenes when the section is personal, reflective, or narrative.

## Blog Image Mix

### Cover

Purpose: make readers feel the article before they understand every mechanism.

Default:

- editorial scene;
- human-scale anchor;
- quiet AI presence;
- strong composition;
- no text;
- no dense maps.

### Thumbnail

Purpose: clickability and title clarity.

Default:

- same world as cover;
- bold text;
- minimal background complexity;
- no small labels outside headline.

### Body Images

Default order:

1. Early thesis image: metaphor or very light comparison.
2. Concrete work example: human-scale scene, not a giant system diagram.
3. Broader workflow: metaphor map, low density.
4. One mechanism image: the cleanest possible diagram.
5. Self-enhancement: symbolic feedback loop, not analytics dashboard.
6. Closing: warm human scene with AI in background.

## Anti-Patterns

Reject images that contain:

- dense chart walls;
- many floating dashboards;
- fake UI text;
- excessive glowing 3D panels;
- generic robot/brain/cloud metaphors;
- random neon network effects;
- one-note dark blue palette;
- "AI command center" visual clichés;
- information density that requires zooming.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Can the image be explained in one sentence?
- [ ] Does it have a human-scale anchor or strong metaphor?
- [ ] Is the AI layer secondary unless this is the one allowed mechanism diagram?
- [ ] Are there no more than three glass/system accents?
- [ ] Does the image have enough negative space?
- [ ] Would it feel comfortable inside a blog, not only as a standalone poster?
- [ ] Does it avoid fake labels, chart clutter, and neon HUD energy?
- [ ] Does it fit Aaron's long-term brand rather than this single article only?

## Application To The AI Operating System Article

The generated v1 set should be treated as a useful proof of capability but too information-heavy.

The v2 outline should use:

- cover: Soft Glass Editorial;
- thumbnail: Soft Glass Editorial with title;
- body image 1: light metaphor for tool vs operating system;
- body image 2: human-scale coding run scene;
- body image 3: metaphor for work leaving the desk;
- body image 4: minimal Notion/memory diagram;
- body image 5: one clean intent-skill-run-review-memory framework;
- body image 6: symbolic garden/feedback loop for self-enhancement;
- body image 7: light creative lab scene, not a dense contact sheet;
- body image 8: warm family/life image.

Only images 4 or 5 may be diagrammatic. The rest should be narrative or metaphorical.
